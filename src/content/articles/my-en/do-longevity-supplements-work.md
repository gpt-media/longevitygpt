If you want the short version: most supplements sold for longevity do not have human evidence that they help you live longer, and several rest entirely on animal or cell studies. A small number of basics have genuine human evidence for healthspan, meaning strength, function, or fixing a deficiency. And the things with the strongest evidence for a longer life are not supplements at all. They are free.

That is the honest verdict. The rest of this article explains why, grades the evidence compound by compound, and tells you the handful of cases where a supplement is actually worth your money.

## The short answer

Longevity is the most over-promised corner of health. A molecule that extends lifespan in mice, worms, or a petri dish gets marketed as if the same is proven in people. It almost never is. Mice are not small humans, and a result in a lab animal is the beginning of a question, not the end of one.

So we split the field into three honest buckets:

1. **Real human healthspan evidence** (creatine, omega-3, correcting a measured vitamin D or magnesium deficiency). These help with strength, function, or filling a genuine gap. None is proven to extend human lifespan.
2. **Preliminary or biomarker-only** (NMN and other NAD+ boosters). They move a number in the blood. Whether that slows aging in humans is unknown.
3. **Mostly marketing for longevity** (resveratrol is the cautionary tale). Strong in animals, repeatedly disappointing in humans.

If you only remember one line, remember this: supplements are the last 5 percent, not the first 50. ## How "it works" gets oversold

Most longevity supplements follow the same story arc. A compound activates a pathway linked to aging (sirtuins, NAD+ metabolism, autophagy). It extends life in a simple organism. A company launches. The pathway is real, but three things usually get lost in the marketing.

First, **species matter**. Calorie restriction and several compounds extend life dramatically in worms and flies, less so in mice, and the human translation is uncertain or absent. Second, **a biomarker is not an outcome**. Raising NAD+ in your blood, or activating an enzyme, is not the same as adding healthy years. Third, **dose and bioavailability matter**. Several compounds that look potent in a dish are barely absorbed or rapidly cleared in people, so the human dose never reaches the levels that worked in the lab.

None of this means the science is fake. It means the gap between "promising mechanism" and "proven in humans" is wide, and longevity marketing routinely papers over it.

## What actually has human evidence

These are the supplements where human trials, not just mechanism, support a real benefit. Notice that the benefit is healthspan (how well you function), not a proven longer lifespan.

### Creatine monohydrate

Strongest case in the category. Creatine is one of the most studied supplements in existence. In older adults, especially when combined with resistance training, it improves muscle strength, lean mass, and functional capacity. Systematic reviews also find benefits for some cognitive measures, with the certainty of evidence rated moderate for memory and lower for other domains like processing speed and executive function.

**Evidence grade: strong human trials for strength and function; moderate for memory.** It is not proven to extend lifespan, but maintaining muscle and strength into older age is one of the better-evidenced ways to stay independent and reduce frailty.

Dose is simple: creatine monohydrate, 3–5 g per day, every day. No loading phase is needed; that just gets you to saturation a few days sooner. Skip the expensive "advanced" forms. Monohydrate is the form the research used. ### Omega-3 (EPA and DHA)

The honest picture is more mixed than the supplement aisle suggests. The clearest, highest-certainty benefit is that EPA and DHA reduce blood triglycerides in a dose-dependent way. For preventing heart attacks, strokes, and death, a large Cochrane review found little or no effect on overall mortality (high-certainty evidence) and only a slight possible reduction in coronary events (low-certainty evidence).

**Evidence grade: strong for lowering triglycerides; mixed and modest at best for cardiovascular events and mortality.** It is most worth considering if you eat little oily fish or have high triglycerides.

Buy on EPA and DHA content, not total fish-oil milligrams. A label can say "1000 mg fish oil" while delivering only a few hundred milligrams of actual EPA plus DHA. ### Vitamin D (only if you are deficient)

This is a correction story, not a longevity story. Trials in generally healthy, replete adults (such as large randomised data on people not selected for deficiency) have not shown that vitamin D supplements prevent heart attacks or strokes, and the overall mortality signal is small at best. The picture is different if you are actually deficient, which is common in people with little sun exposure, darker skin, or who are older.

In Malaysia, where sun exposure is high year-round, it might seem like deficiency is rare. Indoor-heavy lifestyles, sunscreen use, and darker skin tones that require longer exposure to synthesise the same vitamin D can mean many Malaysians still run low. A blood test is the only way to know.

**Evidence grade: strong for correcting deficiency; weak for benefit if you are already replete.** The sensible move is to test, then supplement D3 if you are low, rather than taking high doses blind. ### Magnesium (mainly if intake or levels are low)

Magnesium is essential for hundreds of bodily processes, and older adults and people on certain medications (some blood-pressure drugs, proton-pump inhibitors) are more prone to running low. Correcting a genuine shortfall can help symptoms like cramps, poor sleep, and fatigue. As a lifespan extender in well-nourished people, the evidence is not there.

**Evidence grade: strong for correcting low intake or deficiency; limited as a general longevity supplement.** Form matters for absorption and comfort: glycinate and citrate are well absorbed and gentler, while oxide is cheap but poorly absorbed and more likely to cause loose stools. ## What is mostly marketing (for longevity)

### NMN and other NAD+ boosters

NAD+ is a coenzyme central to energy metabolism, and its levels fall with age, which is the entire pitch. In humans, short trials show NMN reliably raises blood NAD+ and looks safe over the periods studied, with some signals for insulin sensitivity or physical function in specific groups. But raising NAD+ is a biomarker change, not a demonstrated slowing of aging. The lifespan and healthspan headlines come from mice, and long-term human safety data is limited.

**Evidence grade: preliminary in humans (biomarker moves; outcomes unproven); lifespan data is animal-only.** Genuinely interesting, genuinely unproven for the thing it is sold for. Treat it as an experiment you are paying to run on yourself. NMN is available via iHerb, Shopee, and Lazada as an import; expect to pay RM150–350 per month depending on dose and brand (approximate, check current listing). We track it honestly in [does NMN work](/my/articles/does-nmn-work/).

### Resveratrol

The cautionary tale of the whole field. Resveratrol activates sirtuins and extends life in simple organisms, which launched a wave of products. Then the human trials came. After many years and many studies, the results have been largely neutral, a US National Institute on Aging analysis found it did not affect health or longevity in a population study, and resveratrol's low bioavailability in humans means little of it even reaches circulation.

**Evidence grade: animal-only for lifespan; human trials largely neutral.** A textbook example of a mechanism that did not translate. Detail in [resveratrol evidence](/my/articles/resveratrol-evidence/).

## The honest part: free habits outperform the pills

Here is the comparison nobody selling supplements wants front of mind.

- **Physical activity:** strong, consistent human evidence that regular movement is linked to a substantially lower risk of early death, across ages. No supplement is close.
- **Sleep:** poor sleep is tied to worse metabolic, cardiovascular, and cognitive outcomes. Fixing it costs nothing.
- **Diet:** a mostly whole-food, plant-rich pattern with enough protein has far stronger evidence than any single capsule, and it delivers the same nutrients (omega-3s, magnesium) in their best-absorbed form.
- **Not smoking and moderate alcohol:** among the largest, best-established levers on lifespan.

If those are not handled, no stack of capsules will compensate. If they are handled, supplements become a small, optional top-up rather than the strategy.

## So when is a supplement actually worth it?

Three honest cases:

1. **You have a measured deficiency.** Low vitamin D on a blood test, or low magnesium with matching symptoms. Correcting a real gap is where supplements earn their keep.
2. **Your diet cannot realistically cover a nutrient.** For example omega-3 EPA and DHA if you rarely eat oily fish.
3. **There is solid human evidence for a specific goal you care about.** Creatine for strength and function is the cleanest example.

Outside those, most longevity-branded supplements are optional at best and speculative at worst. Buy plain, single-ingredient products from reputable brands available locally at Watsons, Guardian, Caring Pharmacy, BIG Pharmacy, or GNC, or import via iHerb, Shopee, or Lazada. Pick well-absorbed forms, and ignore proprietary "longevity blends" that hide doses.

## Who should talk to a professional first

This article is educational, not medical advice. Speak to a doctor or pharmacist before starting supplements if you are pregnant or breastfeeding, take prescription medication (omega-3, vitamin D, and magnesium can all interact with common drugs), have kidney or liver conditions, or have persistent symptoms like fatigue or cramps that you are tempted to self-treat. Creatine is a good example of why this matters: it is well tolerated by most healthy people, but if you have reduced kidney function or existing renal disease, it can raise your creatinine reading and complicate how your doctor interprets a kidney test, so it is worth flagging before you start. A simple blood test often answers more than a shelf of bottles.

The bottom line stays the same. For living longer, the proven moves are mostly free, a few basic supplements genuinely help your healthspan when there is a real gap, and the glamorous longevity compounds are, for now, a hopeful story told mostly in mice.

So spend your effort where the evidence actually is. Get sleep, training, and diet right first, then fill any measured gaps with plain, well-chosen basics, and treat the rest as optional.
